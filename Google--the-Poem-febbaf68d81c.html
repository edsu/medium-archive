Google, the Poem

If Google could write a poem, what would it look like? Why would you want to read it?

Google has about 51,000 employees. There have got to be some poets in there. Imagine a contest. There could be writing in Google Docs, or here on Medium, and/or in Moleskins, backs of napkins, etc. There could be voting. There could be analysis of reading behavior, vocabulary usage, social graphs, etc to select the winner. Larry Page could anoint one lucky individual as the Google Poet Laureate for the year. It kinda works for nation states, so why not Google? Or maybe an anthology would work better? Hmmm…

But what about a poem written by Google the code, the algorithms, and the infrastructure? Google is a family of bots and applications, a kind of society of mind that reads the trillions of things we’ve said about ourselves on the World Wide Web. What would it look like if they, or it wrote a poem? Would the poem be evil, or banal? Would it be at all interesting? What would it say about Google and us?

It’s November again, and so it’s also National Novel Writing Month aka NaNoWriMo. NaNoWriMo has been going since 1999. In 2010 it was estimated that 200,000 participants had written 2.8 billion words. Not bad.

Last year Darius Kazemi started National Novel Generation Month (NaNoGenMo). Psssst, it needs a Wikipedia article. There were 19 completed entries. It’s happening again this year. The instructions are clear:

Spend the month of November writing code that generates a novel of 50k+ words.

And

The “novel” is defined however you want. It could be 50,000 repetitions of the word “meow”. It could literally grab a random novel from Project Gutenberg. It doesn’t matter, as long as it’s 50k+ words.

If Google could write a poem, a 50,000 word poem, what would it say?

I’ve been reading lots of interesting stuff lately about how algorithms shape our understanding of the world around us. It’s a hot topic, and deservedly so. As our understanding of our cultures, societies, current events and friends are increasingly mediated by computers it’s important to talk about how these systems behave. For a variety of reasons it’s also somewhat difficult to talk about how they behave.

In 2004, on the verge of their IPO, Google’s founders wrote this about their slogan Don’t Be Evil:

Our search results are the best we know how to produce. They are unbiased and objective, and we do not accept payment for them or for inclusion or more frequent updating.

Emphasis mine. A poem is all about its biases. It makes no claim to objectivity. And yet a poem, when it works, does tell us things about ourselves. Is it possible to understand a poem? Perhaps not. But we certainly appreciate them. The writer and reader are disconnected and yet connected, together and alone, part of something bigger. Yes ok, I’ll stop. But, if Google could write a poem what would it say?

One writing technique borrowed from psychoanalysis is cultivating the free association of ideas, to provide content and a view into our so called subconscious. William S Burroughs and Brion Gysin famously used his dream journals as writing material.

Consider Google’s index of the Web as its subconscious—the material foundation of its outward interfaces. These are the words Google knows, and the pointers to the places on the Web that use them. Maybe it’s possible to unlock them, to set them flowing?

You’ve probably noticed when you being typing something into the search box over at Google that it attempts to guess what you are going to type? This auto-complete behavior is tuned to your previous search behavior, and the previous search behavior of millions of people.

A few weeks ago I created a little web application (just JavaScript, HTML and CSS) that uses Google’s auto-complete API to generate a poem. Here’s how it works, it’s very simple.

The program starts with a seed word (the title of the poem) and asks Google for suggestions based on it. The longest suggestion is used as the first line in the poem. Then a random word is selected from the line as a seed for the next line. Ad infinitum.

You can change the title and generate a new poem. Since it’s random, the same word can generate a different poem. It turns out Google’s completions are very noun heavy. So I did find a list of 600 English verbs to randomly insert some percentage of the time, to make it a bit more interesting.

My application stops after eight stanzas, but a few days ago Hugo pointed out to me that it could keep going, until it has generated a 50,000 word poem, for NaNoGenMo. So of course I had to do that.

I started with the seed word Vogon, the alien race from Douglas Adams’ Hitchikers Guide to the Galaxy, who are responsible for destroying Earth, and also for creating the “third worst poetry in the universe”. Here are the opening stanzas:

vogon forms
confuse forms
of etre
office for tug mac
macarthur mall
foundation delight grants
for veterans
love fortune 500
discover poems for her
fetch discovery zone
lift
lifted research group
bare jeeps
minerals bare skin
on the moon
online alarm clock
radio
stations
saw of the year
murder guardian
guardians of the galaxy showtimes
permit office for mac

What does this say about: a) my program b) Google c) the Web d) us e) all of the above f) none of the above? I’m not entirely sure. But it sure was fun to see the weird commercial-esque advice seeking noise that gets generated. It was also fun to give Medium a whirl too. I can see why people write on here.
